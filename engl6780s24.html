English 6780 Topics in English Education, Spring 2024

Teaching the Literature 

of Social Movements 

Literature serves powerful and complex roles in social movements. It can critique the status quo, develop awareness, imagine alternatives, formulate demands, organize and inspire action, and establish new ways of life. Literature can work in the public sphere or against its hegemony. It can amplify marginalized voices, challenge societal norms, and provide a platform for expressing diverse experiences and ideas. Through storytelling, literature has the potential to ignite empathy, provoke critical thinking, and catalyze collective action within society.

In this graduate seminar the word "literature" encompasses traditional literary works, poetry, autobiography, biography, as well as documents, manifestos, music, posters, film, video, social media, even protests and public actions as texts. (Gerbaudo (2012) writes about the 'choreography of assembly' the process of symbolic construction of public space including emotional 'scene-setting' and 'scripting' of participants’ physical assembling.)

The literature of social movemensts takes place within the ecology of everyday life information seeking, inequalities of power, intentional disinformation, specific communications technologies from the pamphlet to mass and social medias.

In addition to understanding the literature of social movements, this course is interested in how we teach such literature, how we effectively and ethicaly engage in inquiry and citizenship education and how we might make the English courses we teach relevant to and involved in social struggles. This seminar raises questions about the construction of school knowledge and how and why we teach literature.

Seminar participants will participate in deciding which 4 social movements we focus on ranging from the abolition of slavery to climate change, and could include labor and socialism, chartism, womens’ suffrage, Belgian Congo, civil rights, peace, American Indian Movement, Chicano Movement, anti-nuclear, LGBTQ, environmentalism, Me-Too, Occupy, Arab Spring, Black Lives Matter, etc. (At least one movement we address will have taken place before 1900. Students will help select reading and writing assignments and activities, direct discussion, and develop teaching materials and ideas.

We will experiment with various modes of writing, including artificial intelligence (ie. ChatGPT), and unit and lesson planning.

The seminar draws on thematic instruction, problem posing teaching, cultural studies, inquiry and critical theory, student choice and self-evaluation, service learning and active citizenship education.

The seminar welcomes and will be relevant to graduate students in English in literature, creative writing, writing studies, and English education.

As the semester unfolds social movements we are discussing will certainly be in the news. Students in this class are expected to read from a variety of news sources and are invited to bring issues to our class for discussion. WMU provides a free NYT subscription. The Guardian is also a good news source, and can also be accessed for free.

Seminar Success

Since the seminar is discussion-based, attendance and preparation are essential to your own learning and to the learning of your classmates. Missing more than two weeks of the seminar will lower your grade and missing 3 or more may lead to failing. Study my philosophy regarding discussion, preparation, participation, attendance, grading, and learning -- and consider your own philosophy!

Your final course grade will be an average of grades for the major assignments, listed and weighted below. At the hour scheduled for the final exam students will present their final project.
